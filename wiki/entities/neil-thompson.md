# Neil Thompson

**Type:** person
**Affiliation:** MIT (research scientist)

## Overview

Neil Thompson is a research scientist at MIT who studies how AI capabilities evolve across the economy. His primary research contribution relevant to this wiki is the **expertise paradox** — the finding that automation's labor market effects depend critically on *which part* of a job is automated. Thompson and colleagues evaluated 40 AI models across thousands of real-world job tasks, each assessed by practitioners in the relevant field, producing one of the more empirically grounded frameworks for predicting how AI transforms specific roles.

Thompson characterizes the current AI capability trajectory as a "rising tide" — steadily climbing rather than a crashing wave — and is skeptical of predictions that work will be entirely transformed by end of 2026. But he stresses that the tide is rising fast enough that policy responses need to begin before the water reaches the knees.

## Key Facts

- Evaluated 40 AI models across thousands of real-world job tasks, each assessed by domain practitioners [[wiki/sources/megamanager-era-how-many-direct-reports-ai-middle-management]]
- Research co-authored with MIT economist David Autor [[wiki/sources/megamanager-era-how-many-direct-reports-ai-middle-management]]
- Core finding: when AI eliminates lower-expertise task components, wages for remaining workers tend to *rise* (fewer workers, doing more of what makes them irreplaceable) [[wiki/sources/megamanager-era-how-many-direct-reports-ai-middle-management]]
- Countercase: when AI targets the expert core of a role (e.g., GPS eliminating taxi driver navigational mastery), wages fall and professional identity hollows out [[wiki/sources/megamanager-era-how-many-direct-reports-ai-middle-management]]
