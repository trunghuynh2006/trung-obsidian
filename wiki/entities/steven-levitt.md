# Steven Levitt

**Type:** person
**Also known as:** Steven D. Levitt

## Overview

Steven D. Levitt is an economist at the University of Chicago known for applying rigorous econometric tools to unconventional questions — crime, cheating, drug markets, parenting, and electoral politics. His central methodological bet: economics is a set of measurement tools, not a subject matter, and can therefore illuminate any corner of human behavior where data exist. His collaboration with journalist Stephen J. Dubner produced *Freakonomics* (2005, expanded 2006), which made applied microeconomics legible to a general audience.

Levitt's signature move is finding natural experiments or unique data sets that isolate causation from correlation — measuring agents selling their own homes vs. client homes, tracking the same two candidates across consecutive races, tracing crime rates back to a birth cohort affected by *Roe v. Wade*. The method consistently finds that the obvious causal story is wrong and a subtler structural story is right.

## Key Facts

- University of Chicago economist; John Bates Clark Medal recipient (2003) for best American economist under 40
- Co-author of *Freakonomics* with Stephen J. Dubner
- Argued legalized abortion was the primary cause of the 1990s US crime drop — 18 years after *Roe v. Wade*, the cohort of highest-risk potential criminals was smaller [[wiki/sources/freakonomics-introduction]]
- Demonstrated with 100,000 Chicago home sales that real estate agents hold their own homes on the market 10 days longer and sell them for 3% more than homes sold for clients [[wiki/sources/freakonomics-introduction]]
