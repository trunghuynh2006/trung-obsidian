# Freakonomics — Introduction: The Hidden Side of Everything

**Source:** [[raw/sources/Freakonomics expanded.pdf]] (pp. 17–29)
**Authors:** Steven D. Levitt & Stephen J. Dubner
**Ingested:** 2026-04-08
**Tags:** #economics #behavioral-economics #incentives #expertise #conventional-wisdom #methodology

## Summary

The introduction to *Freakonomics* lays out the book's worldview through three vignettes and five explicit theses. It is not a book with a single theme; it is a book with a single *method* — applying the tools of economics (measurement, incentive analysis, controlled comparison) to questions those tools are rarely aimed at.

The opening vignette is the 1990s US crime drop. In 1995, criminologist James Alan Fox formally predicted a "superpredator" wave; President Clinton echoed it. Instead, crime fell by half within five years, hitting a 35-year low by 2000. Experts were wrong in both directions: they failed to see the drop coming, and then failed to identify the real cause. The conventional explanations — a roaring economy, gun control laws, innovative policing in New York City — were logical, politically convenient, and false. The actual driver had been set in motion 20 years earlier: *Roe v. Wade* (1973) legalized abortion, and the women most likely to seek one were poor, unmarried, and teenage — the exact demographic whose children would have been statistically most likely to commit crimes. By the early 1990s, those unborn children would have entered their criminal primes. The pool of potential criminals had dramatically shrunk.

The second vignette establishes the expert-incentive problem. Real estate agents have a large informational advantage over their clients. They also have misaligned incentives: a 6% commission sounds large, but after the four-way split (buyer's agent, seller's agent, and each agent's agency), only 1.5% of the sale price reaches the selling agent. The marginal gain from waiting longer on a $300,000 house — holding out for $310,000 — is $9,400 for the seller and $150 for the agent. The agent has no financial incentive to work harder. Using data from 100,000 Chicago-area home sales, Levitt shows agents hold their own homes on the market an average of 10 days longer and sell them for 3% more. Experts do not always use their informational advantage to serve you; they use it to serve themselves.

The third vignette addresses campaign finance. The truism that money wins elections fails under scrutiny. Controlling for candidate appeal across consecutive races between the same two candidates, cutting spending in half loses only 1% of the vote. The candidate who raises more money wins not because of the money — but because appealing candidates both attract donors and attract voters. Money is the effect, not the cause.

Together, the three vignettes illustrate a single diagnostic error: **mistaking correlation for causation, especially when experts have incentives to misidentify the cause.**

## Key Points

- The 1990s US crime drop was not caused by the economy, gun control, or policing. It was caused by *Roe v. Wade* (1973) reducing the cohort of highest-risk children born ~18 years prior. Experts failed to identify this because it was politically inconvenient and required measuring something 20 years in the past.
- Real estate agents demonstrably exploit informational advantage against clients: agents sell their own homes for 3% more and hold them 10 days longer than homes they sell for clients (100,000 Chicago homes data set).
- Campaign money does not cause electoral wins. Candidate appeal causes both fundraising and winning. Same two candidates in consecutive races: doubling or halving spending shifts the vote by only 1%.
- Economics is a science of measurement, not a subject matter. Its tools can be applied to any question.
- **The five Freakonomics theses:**
  1. *Incentives are the cornerstone of modern life.* Ferreting them out is the key to any riddle.
  2. *Conventional wisdom is often wrong.* It is shoddily formed and devilishly hard to see through.
  3. *Dramatic effects often have distant, even subtle causes.* The answer is rarely in front of you.
  4. *Experts use their informational advantage to serve their own agenda.* And that advantage is shrinking daily.
  5. *Knowing what to measure and how to measure it makes a complicated world much less so.*
