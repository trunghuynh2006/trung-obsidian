# As AI Erases the Bottom Rungs of the Corporate Ladder, Some Gen Z Workers Skip the Entry Level to Become Their Own CEOs

**Source:** [[raw/sources/gen-z-entrepreneurs-business-ai.md]]
**Ingested:** 2026-04-27
**Tags:** #ai #gen-z #entrepreneurship #labor #careers

## Summary

This Guardian article argues that Gen Z is entering the labor market just as AI and economic weakness are eroding the traditional entry-level path. The core image is a missing career ladder: recent graduates are struggling to get the first corporate role that would normally teach them skills, build credibility, and lead to advancement. Entry-level work is especially exposed because it often contains routine cognitive tasks that AI can now absorb.

The article balances two sides of the AI labor story. AI threatens junior roles in marketing, coding, customer service, data entry, and analyst work, but the same tools also let some young workers start businesses or prototypes earlier than they otherwise could. Ashley Terrell builds branded-content work after failing to find a marketing job; Suhit Agarwal uses Claude Code in founding-engineer roles; Madison Hsieh uses Cursor to prototype a social app; Celeste Amadon jumps from internships to running Known, an AI-supported dating app.

The article's strongest contribution to this wiki is the concept of [[wiki/concepts/ai-enabled-entrepreneurship]] as adaptation to [[wiki/concepts/entry-level-job-erosion]]. Entrepreneurship is not presented as easy or universal: the source explicitly notes financial risk, funding inequality, long hours, and the continued appeal of stable pay and benefits. But it captures an important shift in sentiment: for some young workers, ownership now feels safer than waiting for institutions to restore the old ladder.

## Key Points

- US hiring has slowed, and unemployment among Americans ages 22-27 is at its highest level since the pandemic, according to the article's cited labor-market sources.
- Entry-level roles are exposed because they contain routine cognitive work that AI can automate or compress.
- A 2025 LinkedIn survey cited in the article found 63% of executives expected AI to replace at least some work done by entry-level employees.
- Stanford Digital Economy Lab research is cited as finding a substantial decline in employment for early-career workers in AI-exposed fields.
- AI creates a countervailing opportunity: low-code and coding-agent tools let young workers prototype, market, and operate projects with less senior support.
- The source's case studies frame entrepreneurship as partly voluntary ambition and partly defensive adaptation to a blocked job market.
- The article stresses limits: most startups fail, capital access is unequal, successful founders often work constantly, and some young entrepreneurs still want stable employment.
