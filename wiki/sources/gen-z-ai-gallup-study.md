# Gen Z AI Attitudes Survey: Gallup / Walton Family Foundation / GSV Ventures (2026)

**Source:** [[raw/sources/gen-z-ai-gallup-study.md]]
**Ingested:** 2026-04-09
**Tags:** #ai #gen-z #labor #public-sentiment #workforce #survey

## Summary

A Gallup survey of 1,500+ Americans ages 14–29, conducted February–March 2026 and released in partnership with the Walton Family Foundation and GSV Ventures, finds that Gen Z's attitudes toward AI have soured markedly since the previous year — even as usage rates remain flat at roughly half the cohort using AI on a daily or weekly basis. The headline number: the share who feel *hopeful* about AI dropped from 27% to 18%, while nearly a third of respondents report feeling *angry* about the technology. Excitement also fell. The most widely reported emotion across all options, however, was curiosity.

The survey captures a cohort in a structurally precarious position: young adults beginning careers or planning them, in a labor market already struggling to absorb recent college graduates, facing an AI wave they believe threatens the professional ambitions they have not yet fulfilled. Working Gen Z-ers are especially skeptical — close to half say AI risks outweigh benefits in the workplace, an 11-point jump from the previous year, with only 15% calling AI a net benefit. This is not a story of rejection: the same respondents acknowledge AI could make them more efficient, and about half of pre-graduates expect to need AI fluency in their future careers. It is a story of credible threat recognition without the social infrastructure to process it.

Researcher Zach Hrynowski of Gallup notes adoption has plateaued: usage is broadly similar to the previous year even as access has widened. The youngest members of Gen Z (ages 14–18) are the most frequent AI users. Older working members are the most skeptical. The gap between frequent use and declining hope suggests the issue is not ignorance of AI tools but direct experience with what those tools imply for their career futures.

## Key Points

- Hope about AI fell from 27% → 18% year-over-year among 14–29-year-olds.
- ~1/3 of respondents say AI makes them feel *angry*; excitement also declined.
- Most common emotion across all options: **curiosity**.
- ~50% use AI daily or weekly — flat from the previous year despite increased access.
- Close to half of working Gen Z say AI risks outweigh workplace benefits (+11 points YoY).
- Only 15% of Gen Z workers call AI a net benefit in the workplace.
- Core concerns: job replacement, creativity/critical thinking atrophy, AI-fueled misinformation, loss of human interaction.
- ~half of pre-graduates expect to need AI fluency in their future careers.
- The youngest members (14–18) are the most frequent AI users.
- Source is a NYT summary of the Gallup survey; no direct access to methodology or crosstabs.
