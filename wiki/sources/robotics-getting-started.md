# This is What Getting Started in Robotics Really Looks Like

**Source:** [[raw/sources/the-complete-guide-to-getting-started]]
**Ingested:** 2026-04-06
**Tags:** #robotics #education #career #learning #beginners

## Summary

A practitioner's guide pushing back on the chaos of "how do I get started in robotics?" advice online. The central argument: robotics has no single entry point because it is genuinely multidisciplinary (mechanical, electrical, software, control, perception, AI), and trying to master all of it upfront guarantees burnout. The right approach is problem-driven learning — build something, break it, fix it, and let the problems you encounter create your learning path.

The piece addresses three audiences: high schoolers (start with competition teams or a kit, build iteratively), university students (pick a core discipline and borrow from the others, stay hands-on), and those considering graduate school (a PhD trains you to answer unanswered questions, not to deploy robots in the field).

The author explicitly rejects roadmaps as confusing order with learning. Robotics learning is non-linear — you loop, revisit, and learn just enough to unblock yourself. A skill tree (varying by role and robot type) is offered as a better mental model. Portfolio matters more than certificates.

## Key Points

- Robotics is still early: unsolved problems include perception under uncertainty, reliable manipulation, energy-efficient actuation, robust autonomy outside controlled environments.
- No single entry point — multidisciplinary by nature. Most roboticists arrive through adjacent fields (ME, EE, CS).
- Fewer than 100 U.S. universities offer a dedicated robotics bachelor's degree.
- Two questions matter more than any roadmap: (1) what stage of education are you at? (2) what do you already know?
- Don't master everything upfront — learn just enough to make something move, sense, or react.
- **Core thesis: follow problems, not roadmaps.** Each problem creates its own personal learning path.
- High school path: competition teams (FIRST, VEX, RoboFest) → basic kit (Arduino, sensors) → iterate → single-board computers → CAD/3D printing.
- University path: pick a core discipline, take cross-discipline electives, stay hands-on.
- Simulation: no setup headaches, faster iteration, cheap/free. Use it, especially early.
- PhD trains you to answer unsolved questions — not to deploy robots in the field.
- "Good roboticists don't follow roadmaps. They follow problems."
- Document everything. Portfolio matters more than certificates.
