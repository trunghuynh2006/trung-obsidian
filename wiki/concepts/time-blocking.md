# Time Blocking

## Definition

The practice of assigning specific, protected windows on a calendar to particular types of work — especially deep work — rather than working reactively through a task list.

## Why It's Necessary

Without time blocking, deep work doesn't happen by default. Meetings, shallow tasks, and interruptions naturally expand to fill undefended calendar space. The act of blocking time makes focus a scheduled commitment rather than a wish.

Time blocking can improve productivity by up to **80%** [[wiki/sources/deep-work-vs-shallow-work]].

## How to Implement

**Step 1 — Classify tasks:** Sort your task list into deep work (cognitively demanding, requires full focus) and shallow work (can be done while distracted).

**Step 2 — Identify your peak hours:** When do you focus best? Morning, midday, evening? Schedule deep work blocks here.

**Step 3 — Block deep work first:** 1–2 deep work tasks per day, in 1–4 hour sessions. Treat these like unmissable appointments.

**Step 4 — Batch shallow work:** 30-minute windows at start and end of day for email/Slack/admin. Specific hours for meetings.

**Step 5 — Protect the blocks:** Mute notifications, DND on phone, Slack status set. Communicate availability to colleagues.

**Step 6 — Leave recovery gaps:** Schedule breaks after deep work sessions; don't fill them with shallow work.

## The One Big Move

A high-leverage addition to time blocking [[wiki/sources/daily-productivity-rituals]]: each morning, name a single task — the **One Big Move** — that makes today count regardless of what else happens. Write it visibly. Every distraction during the day can be evaluated against: "is this more important than my One Big Move?" This prevents afternoon drift when blocks are technically scheduled but attention has fragmented.

## Key Principles

- Focus on **1–2 deep work tasks per day** — not a long list.
- **4–5 hours** is the realistic daily ceiling for deep work quality; block accordingly.
- The blocks must be **flexible** enough to survive real-schedule changes, but **defended** against casual override.
- A **No-Meeting Day** (one full day/week free of meetings) is a high-leverage structural change for teams.
