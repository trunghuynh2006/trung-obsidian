# Attention Residue

## Definition

When you switch from one task to another, part of your attention remains "stuck" on the previous task — the cognitive residue of incomplete or interrupted work. This residue impairs performance on whatever you're currently doing by reducing available cognitive resources.

Coined by Dr. Sophie Leroy (2009) in her research published in *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*.

## The Mechanism

When Task A is still unresolved in your mind while you're working on Task B, your brain is effectively running two threads. The residue from A consumes cognitive resources that should be fully available to B. Result: you process information less carefully, notice fewer errors, make poorer decisions.

Crucially: **the cost applies equally to micro and macro interruptions.** A "quick" glance at your phone carries the same cognitive penalty as a full task switch. Newport's formulation: "If you rarely go more than 10-15 minutes without a 'just check,' you've effectively put yourself in a persistent state of self-imposed cognitive handicap."

## Quantified Impact

From research on interruptions:
- **27% longer** to complete tasks after interruption. [[wiki/sources/deep-work-flow-state]]
- **2× more errors** committed. [[wiki/sources/deep-work-flow-state]]
- **2× more anxiety** experienced. [[wiki/sources/deep-work-flow-state]]
- **23 minutes** to fully refocus after a single interruption. [[wiki/sources/deep-work-flow-state]]
- Average knowledge worker experiences **50–60 interruptions per day** — without structured protection, almost nothing demanding gets done at depth. [[wiki/sources/daily-productivity-rituals]]

## Mitigation Strategies

Leroy's research suggests that taking a few seconds to deliberately **close out** a previous task before switching — reviewing where you left off, noting next steps — can reduce residue. The brain needs to feel the prior task is "parked" before it can fully release it.

Practically: before a deep work block, do a brief shutdown ritual on whatever you were doing last. Write down the next action. This helps the brain let go.
