# Robotics Multidisciplinarity

## Definition

The property that robotics requires deep competence across multiple engineering disciplines simultaneously — mechanical design, electronics, embedded systems, control theory, software, perception, and AI — with no natural single entry point.

## Detail

This is the root cause of most beginner confusion. When someone asks "how do I get started in robotics?", the conflicting answers (learn ROS2 / learn C++ / start with electronics / start with math) are all technically correct because they reflect different entry points into the same large field.

Key disciplines:
- **Mechanical design** — kinematics, dynamics, CAD, fabrication
- **Electronics** — PCBs, sensors, actuators, power
- **Embedded systems** — microcontrollers, firmware, real-time systems
- **Control theory** — feedback control, PID, state estimation
- **Software** — ROS, middleware, systems programming
- **Perception** — computer vision, sensor fusion, SLAM
- **AI / ML** — reinforcement learning, planning, manipulation

A person could spend years in any one of these and still feel behind. This is why the "perfect roboticist" myth (EE + ME + CS + MS + PhD) is not realistic — it describes over a decade of study.

**The practical implication:** pick a core discipline, borrow from the others as needed. There's no wrong entry point, only incomplete ones.

## Evidence & Examples

- Fewer than 100 U.S. universities offer a dedicated robotics bachelor's degree. Most roboticists arrive through adjacent fields (ME, EE, CS). [[wiki/sources/robotics-getting-started]]
- Graduate robotics programs cover kinematics, estimation/control, perception, and planning as core — plus specializations in locomotion, HRI, medical robotics, soft robotics, multi-robot systems. [[wiki/sources/robotics-getting-started]]

## Open Unsolved Problems (as of source)

Real-world robotics still has hard unsolved problems despite impressive demos:
- Perception under uncertainty
- Reliable manipulation / grasping irregular objects
- Energy-efficient actuation
- Robust autonomy outside controlled environments

## Core Skills (synthesized across sources)

Five skills identified as foundational regardless of entry discipline:
1. Math & Science
2. Programming (Scratch → Python → C++)
3. Electronics & Circuits
4. Problem-Solving & Creativity
5. Teamwork & Collaboration [[wiki/sources/robotics-for-beginners-playtolabs]]
