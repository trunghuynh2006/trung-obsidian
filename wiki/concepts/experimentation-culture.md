# Experimentation Culture

## Definition

An organizational norm where small, visible, low-stakes experimentation is treated as part of the work itself, so judgment and confidence compound through practice rather than waiting for perfect plans.

## Detail

In the current source set, experimentation culture is the behavioral layer of AI adoption. The point is not merely to "play with tools." It is to make learning safe, visible, and repeatable before informal habits harden without guidance. Leaders model their own AI use openly, learning is rewarded before ROI is demanded, and experimentation becomes a shared social process rather than an isolated personal one.

This concept sits between [[wiki/concepts/strategic-pacing]], [[wiki/concepts/ai-governance]], and [[wiki/concepts/ai-workforce-readiness]]. Strategic pacing explains why organizations should move before certainty arrives. AI governance explains how to set ownership and guardrails. Experimentation culture explains how people actually learn inside those guardrails: committees reduce isolation, training defines boundaries, peer communities spread working practices, and visible leader participation tells the organization that adaptation is safe.

The current source argues that the real advantage of early AI adoption is not access to a better model. It is the organizational muscle built from repeated reps: better prompts, better skepticism, better judgment, and more shared context. That makes experimentation culture a compounding asset rather than a soft cultural nicety.
