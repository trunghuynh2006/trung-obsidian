# Conventional Wisdom

## Definition

**Conventional wisdom** is the body of beliefs that have become accepted as obviously true through repetition, logical plausibility, or authority — without being subjected to rigorous measurement. The term was popularized by economist John Kenneth Galbraith (*The Affluent Society*, 1958). Freakonomics treats it as the primary obstacle to honest analysis: not deliberate falsehood, but ideas that *seem* true, are politically convenient, and travel frictionlessly from experts to journalists to public without empirical verification.

## Detail

Levitt and Dubner identify a specific failure mechanism: conventional wisdom forms when a theory is:
1. Logically coherent (it *could* be true)
2. Politically convenient or morally satisfying (it *should* be true)
3. Championed by credentialed experts who benefit from its acceptance

Under these conditions, the theory cements as consensus before the data are examined — and once cemented, experts have reputational incentives to maintain it, not challenge it.

The result is that conventional wisdom is "shoddily formed and devilishly difficult to see through" — not because the evidence is hard to find, but because the incentive structure among experts actively resists finding it.

## Evidence & Examples

- **Crime drop of the 1990s**: conventional wisdom attributed it to the economy, gun control, and policing. All plausible. None primary. The actual cause (abortion legalization 18 years prior) was politically inconvenient and required measuring something two decades in the past. Crime-drop experts cited it zero times [[wiki/sources/freakonomics-introduction]].
- **Campaign finance**: CW holds that money wins elections. Controlling for candidate appeal, spending barely matters (1% vote shift per doubling/halving of spend). The CW gets causation exactly backwards [[wiki/sources/freakonomics-introduction]].
- **Expert consensus on crime wave (1995)**: criminologist James Alan Fox formally predicted a 15–100% increase in teen homicides. Instead, the teen murder rate fell 50% within five years [[wiki/sources/freakonomics-introduction]].
