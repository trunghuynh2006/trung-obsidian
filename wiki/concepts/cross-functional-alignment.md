# Cross-Functional Alignment

## Definition

The deliberate integration of historically siloed departments — especially HR, IT, and Finance — around shared data, shared metrics, and shared accountability for AI transformation outcomes.

## Detail

In most organizations, CHRO, CIO, and CFO have operated in separate fiefdoms with different priorities, vocabularies, and success metrics. This structure worked acceptably in stable environments. Under AI-accelerated change, it becomes a liability: AI's effects on people, systems, and financial planning cannot be managed independently without producing contradictory decisions and missed opportunities.

The problem is not goodwill. CHROs, CIOs, and CFOs often want to collaborate. The structural barriers are:
- **Data misalignment**: HR data on workforce composition, performance, and turnover sits separately from financial data on spending and revenue. Even shared definitions like "Total Cost of Workforce" mean different things to HR and finance. Poor data quality from this misalignment costs companies 15–25% of annual revenue (MIT Sloan).
- **Metric disconnect**: HR reports turnover figures; finance reports costs. Neither alone answers the question that matters: does this workforce investment produce better business outcomes?
- **Decision speed mismatch**: as AI accelerates decisions, the old model of waiting for a quarterly report before acting becomes untenable. Frontline managers need insights in real time — which requires pre-aligned data and agreed-upon interpretation.

The prescription from the current source set has three steps:
1. **Break cultural barriers first** — agree on a shared North Star (often organizational survival under competitive AI pressure); change the identity of HR from support function to strategic driver
2. **Connect talent with outcomes** — build or procure a unified data layer that links workforce data, financial data, and operational data; standardize definitions across departments
3. **Solve the last-mile gap** — use AI tools to deliver plain-language, real-time insights to frontline managers rather than burying them in dashboards

Moderna's structural response (merging CHRO and CIO into one role) is the most extreme example — eliminating the alignment problem by eliminating the boundary.

## Evidence & Examples

- 50% of executives cite organizational/cultural barriers as the #1 AI adoption blocker (HKU survey) [[wiki/sources/whats-ais-real-failure-no-ones-actually-in-charge]]
- A bank used aligned performance + CRM data to avoid mass layoffs: found that 3–5 year support staff had exceptional customer satisfaction scores, contradicting the cost-cutting case for replacement [[wiki/sources/whats-ais-real-failure-no-ones-actually-in-charge]]
- Moderna merged HR and IT leadership entirely: CHRO became Chief People and Digital Technology Officer [[wiki/sources/whats-ais-real-failure-no-ones-actually-in-charge]]
- Poor data quality from siloed systems costs 15–25% of annual revenue (MIT Sloan) [[wiki/sources/whats-ais-real-failure-no-ones-actually-in-charge]]

## Contradictions

The Moderna example is presented as a positive model, but the source (a vendor) has an interest in making org redesign sound necessary to unlock its product. No independent data compares governance model types against each other for AI outcomes. The "merged HR+IT" model is explicitly labeled rare even by the article.
