# Shallow Work

## Definition

"Logistical and administrative duties that can be done while distracted, easily replicated, and usually not creating new value." — Reclaim.ai / Newport framework.

Shallow work is the operational undercurrent of professional life. It's necessary but low-leverage — it keeps things running but doesn't move the needle.

## Examples

- Email and chat (Slack, Teams)
- Meeting attendance (especially status updates and check-ins)
- Data entry and report pulling
- Checking social media
- Administrative coordination

## Why It Matters

The problem isn't that shallow work exists — it's that without deliberate scheduling it expands to fill all available time. Professionals spend the majority of their days in shallow work, ending each week without the heads-down time needed for high-value output.

Statistics [[wiki/sources/deep-work-vs-shallow-work]]:
- Only **11.2 hours/week** of truly productive work on average.
- **2 hours/day** wasted on unproductive tasks.
- **24.5%** of a 40-hour workweek on unproductive task work (emails, Slack).
- Only **53.5%** of planned tasks completed per week despite a 47.6-hour average workweek.

## The Deep/Shallow Test

Before scheduling a task, ask:
- Can I do this while distracted?
- Does this require my full cognitive capability?
- Will this create durable output or just maintain status quo?

If the answers are yes / no / maintain — it's shallow work. Schedule it accordingly.

## Management Strategy

Newport's Rule: **Drain the shallows** — batch shallow work into designated windows rather than allowing it to scatter through the day. This protects contiguous time for deep work.

Practical tactics [[wiki/sources/deep-work-vs-shallow-work]]:
- 30-minute catch-up windows at start and end of day for email/Slack.
- Set meetings to specific hours; create No-Meeting Days.
- Decline or shorten the 71% of meetings that are unproductive.
- Sync Slack status to calendar to reduce ad-hoc interruptions.
