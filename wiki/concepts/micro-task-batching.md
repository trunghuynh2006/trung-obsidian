# Micro-Task Batching

## Definition

The practice of capturing all small, quick tasks (micro-tasks) into a dedicated queue as they arrive — rather than handling them immediately — then processing the entire queue in two dedicated 25–30 minute batch windows per day.

## What Are Micro-Tasks?

Individually tiny but collectively enormous: short message replies, document approvals, meeting confirmations, minor calendar updates, quick email forwards, adding items to shared lists. Each takes 60–180 seconds. Together, for most knowledge workers, they consume **90 minutes to 3 hours per day** — scattered in fragments across every other activity.

## Why Batching Matters

Micro-tasks handled on arrival create constant context switching. Each one is a micro-interruption that generates attention residue — partial cognitive threads left behind even after the 90-second task is "done." The cumulative effect: deep work is never truly uninterrupted; focus is persistently fragmented.

Batching creates three clean separations [[wiki/sources/daily-productivity-rituals]]:
- **Deep work blocks** become genuinely distraction-free — micro-tasks have their own container.
- **Communication windows** handle actual communication — not overloaded with tiny admin.
- **Micro-task batches** process everything else — in focused, purposeful bursts.

Three containers. Zero cross-contamination.

## Implementation

1. Create a micro-task inbox (a tag in your task manager, a dedicated list, or a physical notepad).
2. For every incoming micro-task: capture it to the inbox. Do not handle it immediately.
3. Schedule two 25–30 min batch windows per day (e.g., mid-morning after deep work, and mid-afternoon).
4. Process the entire queue in each window — reply, approve, confirm, forward, close.
5. Nothing in the queue gets handled outside the batch windows.

**Impact is typically immediate and visible within 3 days of implementation.**

## Side Effect: Async Norm Setting

Batching naturally establishes a predictable 2–4 hour response window for non-urgent items. This sets an async communication norm without requiring an awkward team conversation — colleagues learn when to expect responses, and the urgency of "answer now" pressure dissipates.
